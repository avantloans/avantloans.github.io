Could Kamala Harris Be the Key to Student Debt Cancellation?

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Student debt in the United States has ballooned into a crisis, with over 45 million borrowers collectively owing more than $1.7 trillion. For years, activists, lawmakers, and borrowers have pushed for widespread student loan forgiveness, but progress has been slow. Now, as the Biden administration faces mounting pressure to act, Vice President Kamala Harris is emerging as a pivotal figure in the fight for debt cancellation. Could she be the key to unlocking relief for millions of Americans?

The Growing Student Debt Crisis

The burden of student loans has reached unprecedented levels, affecting not just recent graduates but also older generations who are still paying off decades-old debt.

Who’s Affected?

  • Young professionals struggling to buy homes or start families.
  • Low-income borrowers, particularly people of color, who face higher default rates.
  • Parents and grandparents who took out loans to support their children’s education.

Why Hasn’t It Been Solved?

Despite campaign promises, broad student debt cancellation has stalled due to:
- Legal and political hurdles (Can the president cancel debt without Congress?).
- Opposition from conservatives who argue it’s unfair to taxpayers.
- Concerns about inflation in an already shaky economy.

Kamala Harris’s Role in the Fight

As Vice President, Harris doesn’t have direct authority over student loans—but she wields significant influence. Here’s why she could be crucial:

1. Her History with Student Debt Reform

Before becoming VP, Harris was a vocal advocate for education reform. In the Senate, she co-sponsored bills like the Debt-Free College Act, which aimed to make public colleges tuition-free. She has repeatedly called for at least $50,000 in student loan forgiveness per borrower—far more than Biden’s current proposals.

2. Her Influence in the White House

Harris has been tasked with leading key initiatives, from voting rights to broadband access. If Biden decides to expand student debt relief, she could be the one pushing for bolder action behind the scenes.

3. The Political Calculus

With midterms approaching and Democrats needing to energize young voters, Harris could be the administration’s bridge to progressive activists. Canceling student debt could be a major win—but only if done at a meaningful scale.

What’s Standing in the Way?

While Harris may be an ally, there are still obstacles:

Legal Uncertainty

Can Biden cancel debt via executive order? The Education Department has explored options, but any large-scale forgiveness could face lawsuits from Republicans.

Public Opinion

Not everyone supports blanket forgiveness. Some argue it’s a handout to the privileged, while others worry it doesn’t address the root problem: skyrocketing tuition costs.

Economic Concerns

Forgiving billions in debt could have ripple effects—good and bad. While it would free up spending for millions, critics warn it might worsen inflation.

What Could Real Relief Look Like?

If Harris and Biden move forward, here are some possible approaches:

Option 1: Broad Cancellation

  • $10,000 per borrower (Biden’s current stance).
  • $50,000 or more (progressive demand).

Option 2: Targeted Relief

  • Income caps (only those earning under $125K qualify).
  • Forgiveness for public service workers.

Option 3: Reform + Cancellation

  • Cap future interest rates.
  • Make community college free to prevent future debt.

The Bottom Line

Kamala Harris may not be the sole decision-maker, but her voice could tip the scales. If the White House wants to make history—and secure a crucial voting bloc—student debt cancellation might be the move. The question is: Will they go big enough?

For now, millions of borrowers are watching—and waiting.
