What Is a Good APR for a Personal Loan?

Home / Blog / Blog Details

When it comes to personal loans, the Annual Percentage Rate (APR) is one of the most critical factors to consider. It determines how much you’ll pay in interest and fees over the life of the loan. But what exactly is a "good" APR for a personal loan? The answer depends on several factors, including your credit score, the lender, and broader economic conditions like inflation and Federal Reserve policies.

Understanding APR: More Than Just Interest

APR represents the total cost of borrowing, including interest rates and any additional fees (e.g., origination fees, processing charges). Unlike a simple interest rate, APR gives you a more accurate picture of what you’ll actually pay.

How Is APR Calculated?

Lenders consider multiple factors when determining your APR:
- Credit Score: Borrowers with excellent credit (720+) typically qualify for the lowest rates.
- Loan Term: Shorter terms often come with lower APRs but higher monthly payments.
- Economic Climate: Rising inflation or Fed rate hikes can push APRs higher across the board.

What’s Considered a Good APR in 2024?

As of 2024, the average APR for a personal loan ranges from 6% to 36%, depending on the borrower’s creditworthiness. Here’s a general breakdown:

For Excellent Credit (720+ FICO)

  • APR Range: 6% – 12%
  • Example: A $10,000 loan at 8% APR over 3 years costs ~$1,292 in interest.

For Good Credit (680–719)

  • APR Range: 10% – 18%
  • Example: The same $10,000 loan at 15% APR would cost ~$2,423 in interest.

For Fair/Poor Credit (Below 680)

  • APR Range: 18% – 36%
  • Example: At 25% APR, the total interest jumps to ~$4,236.

Why APR Matters More Than Ever in 2024

1. Inflation and Rising Interest Rates

The Federal Reserve’s aggressive rate hikes to combat inflation have directly impacted personal loan APRs. Borrowers today face higher rates than they would have in 2020–2021.

2. The Debt Crisis

With credit card APRs soaring above 20%, many consumers are turning to personal loans for debt consolidation. A "good" APR in this context is one that’s significantly lower than your current credit card rates.

3. Lender Competition

Online lenders (e.g., SoFi, LendingClub) often offer lower APRs than traditional banks, especially for well-qualified borrowers. Shopping around is crucial.

How to Secure the Best APR

Improve Your Credit Score

  • Pay bills on time.
  • Reduce credit utilization below 30%.
  • Dispute errors on your credit report.

Compare Multiple Lenders

  • Use prequalification tools (soft credit checks) to compare rates without hurting your score.
  • Watch out for predatory lenders targeting subprime borrowers.

Negotiate Fees

Some lenders may waive origination fees (1%–8% of the loan amount) if you have strong credit or an existing relationship with them.

Red Flags: When an APR Is Too Good to Be True

  • "Guaranteed Approval" Loans: Often come with sky-high APRs or hidden fees.
  • Short-Term Payday Loans: APRs can exceed 400%, trapping borrowers in cycles of debt.
  • Unlicensed Lenders: Always verify a lender’s credentials through the CFPB or state regulators.

The Bottom Line

A "good" APR is relative, but in today’s high-rate environment, anything below 15% is competitive for borrowers with good credit. For those with lower scores, focusing on improving credit or seeking secured loan options (e.g., using collateral) could lead to better rates. Stay informed, shop smart, and never settle for the first offer you receive.
