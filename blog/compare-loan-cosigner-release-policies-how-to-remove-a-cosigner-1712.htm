Compare Loan Cosigner Release Policies: How to Remove a Cosigner

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Navigating the world of loans can be tricky, especially when you’ve needed a cosigner to secure financing. Whether it’s for a student loan, auto loan, or mortgage, having a cosigner can help you qualify for better terms—but what happens when you no longer need one? Removing a cosigner isn’t always straightforward, and policies vary widely between lenders. In this guide, we’ll break down the key differences in cosigner release policies and provide actionable steps to free your cosigner from financial responsibility.

Why Cosigner Release Matters

Cosigners take on significant risk when they agree to back a loan. If the primary borrower defaults, the cosigner’s credit score takes a hit, and they may even face legal action. Given today’s economic uncertainties—rising inflation, fluctuating interest rates, and job market instability—many cosigners are eager to step back once the borrower demonstrates financial stability.

The Impact of Economic Trends on Cosigner Release

With student loan debt in the U.S. surpassing $1.7 trillion and auto loan delinquencies on the rise, lenders are tightening their policies. Some have made cosigner release more difficult, while others offer streamlined processes to encourage responsible borrowing. Understanding these trends can help borrowers strategize their approach.

Types of Loans with Cosigner Release Options

Not all loans allow for cosigner release, and those that do often have strict requirements. Below, we compare policies across common loan types.

Student Loans

Federal student loans rarely require cosigners, but private student loans often do. Here’s how major lenders handle cosigner release:

  • Sallie Mae: Requires 12 consecutive on-time payments, a good credit score, and proof of income.
  • Discover: Allows release after 36 months of timely payments and a credit check.
  • SoFi: No formal cosigner release policy—borrowers must refinance to remove a cosigner.

Auto Loans

Auto lenders are generally more flexible, but policies vary:

  • Capital One: No cosigner release—refinancing is the only option.
  • Bank of America: May release a cosigner after 12-24 months of consistent payments.
  • Credit Unions: Often have borrower-friendly policies, sometimes allowing release after just six months.

Personal Loans

Most personal loans don’t offer cosigner release, meaning refinancing is typically the only way to remove a cosigner.

Step-by-Step Guide to Removing a Cosigner

If your lender allows cosigner release, follow these steps:

1. Review Your Loan Agreement

Check for any clauses related to cosigner release. Some lenders bury these details in fine print.

2. Build Your Credit and Payment History

Most lenders require:
- A strong credit score (usually 670 or higher).
- A history of on-time payments (often 12-48 months).

3. Submit a Formal Request

Contact your lender and ask for their cosigner release application. Be prepared to provide:
- Proof of income.
- Recent credit reports.
- A written statement explaining why you no longer need a cosigner.

4. Explore Refinancing as a Backup

If your lender denies your request, refinancing with a new loan in your name alone may be the best alternative.

Common Roadblocks and How to Overcome Them

Strict Income Requirements

Some lenders demand that borrowers earn a certain multiple of the monthly payment. If you don’t meet this threshold, consider:
- Taking on a side hustle.
- Waiting until your salary increases.

Hidden Fees

A few lenders charge processing fees for cosigner release. Always ask about costs upfront.

Lender Resistance

If your lender refuses to cooperate, escalate the issue to a supervisor or file a complaint with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).

The Future of Cosigner Release Policies

As fintech companies disrupt traditional lending, some are introducing more flexible cosigner release options. Blockchain-based lending platforms, for example, are experimenting with smart contracts that automatically release cosigners once conditions are met.

For now, though, borrowers must navigate existing policies carefully. By understanding lender requirements and proactively managing credit, you can successfully remove a cosigner and achieve full financial independence.
