Personal Loan Closing Costs: Are There Any?

Home / Blog / Blog Details

When it comes to personal loans, many borrowers focus on interest rates and repayment terms—but what about closing costs? Unlike mortgages, personal loans don’t always come with hefty fees at signing. However, that doesn’t mean they’re entirely fee-free. Understanding personal loan closing costs can save you from unexpected expenses and help you make smarter financial decisions.

What Are Personal Loan Closing Costs?

Closing costs are fees charged by lenders to cover the administrative expenses of processing a loan. For mortgages, these costs can include appraisal fees, title insurance, and origination fees. But with personal loans, the structure is often simpler—though not always free.

Common Fees Associated with Personal Loans

While personal loans typically have fewer closing costs than mortgages, here are some fees you might encounter:

  1. Origination Fees

    • Many lenders charge an origination fee, usually between 1% and 8% of the loan amount.
    • This fee compensates the lender for processing your application and underwriting the loan.

  2. Prepayment Penalties

    • Some lenders impose fees if you pay off your loan early.
    • Always check the fine print to avoid surprises.

  3. Late Payment Fees

    • Missing a due date can trigger additional charges, increasing your overall loan cost.

  4. Application or Processing Fees

    • Less common, but some lenders charge a flat fee just for applying.

  5. Check Processing Fees

    • If you make payments via paper check, some lenders may charge a small fee.

Why Some Personal Loans Have No Closing Costs

Unlike mortgages, personal loans are often unsecured, meaning they don’t require collateral. This reduces the need for appraisals, title searches, or other costly steps. Many online lenders and credit unions offer personal loans with no origination fees or closing costs, especially to borrowers with strong credit.

The Role of Credit Scores in Closing Costs

Your credit score plays a significant role in whether you’ll face closing costs:

  • Excellent Credit (720+) – More likely to qualify for no-fee loans.
  • Fair to Good Credit (580-719) – May incur origination fees.
  • Poor Credit (Below 580) – Higher fees or even loan denial.

How to Avoid Unnecessary Fees

Nobody wants to pay extra for a loan. Here’s how to minimize or eliminate closing costs:

1. Compare Multiple Lenders

  • Online lenders, banks, and credit unions have different fee structures.
  • Use loan comparison tools to find the best deal.

2. Negotiate with the Lender

  • Some lenders may waive fees if you have a strong financial profile.

3. Opt for a Shorter Loan Term

  • Longer terms may have higher overall costs, even if monthly payments are lower.

4. Read the Fine Print

  • Always review the loan agreement before signing.

The Impact of Rising Interest Rates on Personal Loans

With the Federal Reserve increasing interest rates to combat inflation, borrowing costs have risen across the board. This affects personal loans in two ways:

  1. Higher APRs – Even if closing costs remain low, the interest you pay over time could be significantly higher.
  2. Tighter Lending Standards – Banks may impose stricter requirements, making no-fee loans harder to secure.

Alternative Options If Closing Costs Are Too High

If you’re facing steep fees, consider alternatives:

  • Credit Cards with 0% APR Promotions – Useful for short-term financing.
  • Home Equity Loans (if applicable) – May have closing costs but lower interest rates.
  • Peer-to-Peer Lending – Sometimes offers competitive terms with minimal fees.

Final Thoughts Before You Borrow

While personal loans generally have fewer closing costs than mortgages, they’re not always free. Always ask lenders for a full breakdown of fees before committing. By doing your research and comparing offers, you can secure a loan that fits your budget without unnecessary expenses.
